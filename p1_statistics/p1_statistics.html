P1: Test a Perceptual Phenomenon

¶

In this project, I will be analyzing two paired Stroop task samples.

1a) Independent variable

In a stroop task experiment, the independent variable is ink color, or more specifically whether or not the ink color of the listed words match the written color name. When the color of the ink and the text of the word match we call it the congruent word condition, and the incongruent word condition when the ink color and word text are different. We can call this binary variable Ink Match (where the only possible values are Congruent and Incongruent).

1b) Dependent variable

The dependent variable is time it takes to name the ink colors, in equally sized lists. Each participant will go through the experiment and record a time for both conditions, starting with the congruent condition.

2a) Hypotheses

The null hypothesis for this project is that Ink Match has no effect on the mean time it takes people to solve a Stroop task.

The alternative hypothesis is that a positive Ink Match (the congruent condition) lowers the mean time it takes the people to solve the tasks.

Mathematically, we can display the hypotheses like this:

$M_1 =$ Population mean time of the congruent condition

$M_2 =$ Population mean time of the incongruent condition

$H_0: M_1 = M_2$

$H_A: M_1 < M_2$

If the statistical tests for the sample show a statistical significance supporting the alternative hypothesis, we can with confidence reject the null hypothesis for the population. An alpha level of 0.05 will be used when testing for statistical significance.

2b) Statistical test

A dependent two sample T-test will be used for this task, as the task has a repeated measures design. This means that the same subjects takes both tests, and their results are recorded for comparison. Although the alternative hypothesis is that mean time will increase in the Incongruent condition, I will still perform a two-tailed T-test as it is technically possible for the mean time to decrease instead.

As the test will be performed on the differences between the samples, I assume the distribution of differences will be close to normal. I also assume there are no extreme outliers among the sample differences.

Another option would be to use repeated measures ANOVA. As the independent variable only has two levels in the stroop task, I have decided to use the paired T-test instead.

As mentioned earlier, I will perform a paired t-test to test for statistical significance of the samples. I will set the alpha level at 0.05. If significant, I will also measure the effect size using Cohen's d.

In my own experience, the text of the word seems to be a larger factor when naming the color than whether the ink color and word match. In other words, I read words faster than I find words for colors. This seems to match the findings of John Ridley Stroop's orginal study (Stroop, 1935). He also tested the reading speed of a condition not included in the test samples above, namely names of colors written in black ink. In Stroop's study, the effect of having to read words naming a color different than its print was relatively minor, averaging only 5.6% slower than for reading names of colors written in black ink, while having to name the ink color of words being the print of a word naming a different color (the incongruent words condition) caused an increase of 74.3% over the average time for naming colors printed in squares. He considered this evidence for "(...) the associations that have been formed between the word stimuli and the reading response are evidently more effective than those that have been formed between the color stimuli and the naming response."

In psychology, what is referred to as an emotional stroop test is sometimes used. In such a test the participant is supposed to say the ink color of various non-color words with different ink colors. The words are divided into two main categories, emotional and neutral words, where the emotional words are divided into the sub-categories positive and negative words. The idea is that emotional words will have a slowing effect on naming the ink color (Dresler et al. 2008).
